Halo Propulsion

Background: The Halo propulsion team is led by myself and one other member. We are the smallest of all sub-teams and fluctuate between two and five members, total.

Scope: Propulsion is responsible for design, part selection, and justification of all propellant systems and the engine. This means making final decisions for propellants, pressures, geometries, etc. This includes selection of: regulators, valves, feed lines, propellant tanks, injector, combustion chamber, fitting assemblies, and throttle valves.

System Architecture

Propellants

Propellants were selected by the following considerations, in order of importance: practicality and availability, safety in handling, and finally, combustion properties.

Practicality and availability is of highest importance to us because of our limited ability to safely store and transport propellants. This immediately excludes all cryogenic propellants.

Limited access to safety training and equipment means a relatively non-toxic fuel that can be handled with simple eye and skin protection is favored.

Performance is of smallest importance because we are not as limited by weight due to the VTVL flight objective. Because of the previous requirements, as well as its heritage in amateur liquid propellant rocketry, Nitrous Oxide (N2O) is the obvious choice.

For fuel, by our previous criteria, our options are limited to petroleum fuels or alcohol fuels. The performance of each was evaluated using the NASA Chemical Equilibrium with Applications (CEA) tool. Using the python package RocketCEA, the following figure was created.

Alcohol fuels were chosen for their performance at lower temperature, and Ethanol is our final selection for its slightly increased performance over IPA.

Pressure-Fed

Our propellants are fed by pressuring the ullage above a piston with high pressure gas as seen on the left of the propellant tank figure below. Both propellant tanks are pressurized this way. Pressurizing N2O ensures N2O will be a liquid in our feed lines and through our injector. This way, pressure drops due to throttle valves and line losses do not lead to a two-phase flow and therefore the density is only a function of pressure and temperature.

Component Design

Throttle Valves

VTVL presupposes the ability to throttle the liquid propellant engine. Several studies have done tests on variable area cavitating venturi (VACV) valves to control mass flow rate of propellants. Also, VACVs act as both valves and dampers of propellant system vibrations. Our VACVs are designed from the paper Application of variable area cavitating venturi as a dynamic flow controller by Tian et. al. The design allows for the mass flow of the entire feed line to be controlled by one valve as well as allowing for mass flow to vary nearly linearly with pintle stroke.

To validate our designs before manufacturing, I did a mixture analysis using ANSYS Fluent and structural analysis using ANSYS Mechanical.

The results of the simulation show cavitation, but present surprising evidence showing that flow is controlled in the diverging section, and not the throat. I am still resolving issues in the simulation to determine what the range of ΔPs is that cause a constant mass flow. I am also considering geometries with longer throats.

Propellant Tanks

Propellant tanks were designed using Half Cat Rocketry's pressure vessel design guide, Half Cat Sim, and the Parker O-Ring Handbook.

The most delicate part of the tank design is the bulkhead. Due to the large diameter, excess thickness contributes significantly to weight. I used ANSYS Mechanical to determine what thickness the bulkhead can be. To seal the bulkhead, I followed the Parker O-Ring handbook recommendations for static seals. O-Ring material compatibility was also considered. Viton or Buna-N were selected based on availability, with Viton preferred whenever available.

Air Actuated Ball Valves

Our larger propellant on/off valves require torque that reasonably sized servo motors cannot provide. Instead, air actuated on/off valves were selected. Off-the-shelf solutions were not found to be satisfactory. I designed a simple mount to connect our ball valves and air actuators such that it could be bent up from thin sheet metal.
